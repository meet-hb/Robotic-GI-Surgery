Planning for Robotic Gi Surgery

Planning for robotic surgery involves both preoperative preparation and in-depth surgical planning using advanced technology. Patient-specific planning uses preoperative imaging to customize the procedure, while logistical and team-based planning includes staff training, equipment setup, and a review of surgeon preferences. Robotic surgery planning helps ensure precision, efficiency, and safety by detailing the surgical steps and robotic setup in advance.

Preoperative and Patient-Specific Planning

  • Imaging and simulation: High-resolution imaging like CT or MRI scans is used with specialized software to create a 3D model of the patient's anatomy.
  • Customized procedure: The surgical plan is customized to the patient's unique anatomy, allowing surgeons to simulate and rehearse the procedure beforehand.
  • Patient preparation: Patients must follow specific pre-operative instructions, such as fasting and potentially a bowel prep, as outlined on this Inova page.

Principles of Port Placements

The principles of port placement are anatomical landmarks, the "baseball diamond" or triangulation method, and ergonomics. Anatomical landmarks ensure safety and access, while triangulation creates a stable working triangle for instruments to achieve depth perception. Ergonomics are critical for surgeon comfort, enabling natural movements and minimizing conflicts between instruments.

Key Principles

  • Safety First (Anatomical Consideration): The most critical principle is avoiding injury to major vessels (aorta, iliac vessels, inferior epigastric arteries), bowel, and bladder during initial entry and subsequent port placement.
  • Alternative sites like Palmer's point (left upper quadrant, 3 cm below the costal margin in the midclavicular line) are used in cases of prior umbilical surgeries or suspected adhesions.
  • Secondary ports should be placed under direct vision to identify and avoid the inferior epigastric vessels which run vertically along the anterior abdominal wall.
